2:27:1247:28:14:72:540:588:12:Times:12:Times:We document that variations in government purchases generate a rise in consumption, the real and
2:28:1343:14:14:72:540:574:12:Times:12:Times:the product wage, and a fall in the markup. This evidence is robust across alternative empirical methodologies
2:29:1453:14:14:72:540:560:12:Times:12:Times:used to identify innovations in government spending (structural VAR vs. narrative approach). Simultaneously
2:30:1560:14:14:72:540:546:12:Times:12:Times:accounting for these facts is a formidable challenge for a neoclassical model, which relies on the wealth
2:31:1665:14:14:72:540:532:12:Times:12:Times:effect on labor supply as the main channel of transmission of unproductive government spending shocks.
2:32:1767:14:14:72:540:518:12:Times:12:Times:The goal of this paper is to explore further the role of the wealth effects in the transmission of government
2:33:1876:14:14:72:540:504:12:Times:12:Times:spending shocks. To this end, we build an otherwise standard business cycle model with price rigidity,
2:34:1978:14:14:72:540:490:12:Times:12:Times:in which preferences can be consistent with an arbitrarily small wealth effect on labor supply, and
2:35:2077:14:14:72:540:476:12:Times:12:Times:highlight that such effect is linked to the degree of complementarity between consumption and hours.
2:36:2177:14:14:72:540:462:12:Times:12:Times:We show that the model is able to match our empirical evidence on the effects of government spending
2:37:2277:14:14:72:539:448:12:Times:12:Times:shocks remarkably well. This happens when the preferences are such that the positive wealth effect on
2:38:2378:14:14:72:541:434:12:Times:12:Times:labor supply is small and therefore the negative wealth effect on consumption is, somewhat counterintuitively,
2:39:2488:14:42:72:99:420:12:Times:12:Times:large.
